Spanish Grammar Guide

In Spanish, nouns are either masculine or feminine. Generally:

  • Nouns ending in -o are masculine (e.g., el libro - the book).
  • Nouns ending in -a are feminine (e.g., la casa - the house).

However, there are exceptions, so it's essential to learn each noun's gender.

Articles must agree in gender and number with the noun they accompany:

  • Definite articles:
    • Masculine singular: el
    • Feminine singular: la
    • Masculine plural: los
    • Feminine plural: las
  • Indefinite articles:
    • Masculine singular: un
    • Feminine singular: una
    • Masculine plural: unos
    • Feminine plural: unas

Subject pronouns in Spanish are:

  • yo - I
  • tú - you (informal singular)
  • él/ella/usted - he/she/you (formal singular)
  • nosotros/nosotras - we (masculine/feminine)
  • vosotros/vosotras - you all (informal plural, used in Spain)
  • ellos/ellas/ustedes - they (masculine/feminine), you all (formal plural)

Spanish verbs are categorized based on their infinitive endings: -ar, -er, and -ir. Each category follows specific conjugation patterns. For example, the present tense conjugation of hablar (to speak) is:

  • yo hablo - I speak
  • tú hablas - you speak
  • él/ella/usted habla - he/she/you speak
  • nosotros/nosotras hablamos - we speak
  • vosotros/vosotras habláis - you all speak
  • ellos/ellas/ustedes hablan - they/you all speak

Adjectives in Spanish must agree in gender and number with the nouns they describe. Typically:

  • Masculine singular: chico alto (tall boy)
  • Feminine singular: chica alta (tall girl)
  • Masculine plural: chicos altos (tall boys)

Some adjectives remain the same for both genders, such as inteligente (intelligent).

Common Spanish prepositions include:

  • a - to, at
  • de - of, from
  • en - in, on
  • por - by, through
  • para - for
  • con - with

Both ser and estar mean "to be," but they are used differently:

  • Ser is used for permanent traits, identity, and time: Ella es doctora. (She is a doctor.)
  • Estar is used for temporary states and locations: Ella está cansada. (She is tired.)

Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition:

  • Direct object pronouns: me, te, lo/la, nos, os, los/las
  • Indirect object pronouns: me, te, le, nos, os, les

Example:

Juan me dio un libro. (Juan gave me a book.)
